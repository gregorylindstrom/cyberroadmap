// 01 · Scholarships

Free money, real obligations.

Cybersecurity scholarships can pay full tuition plus a stipend — sometimes $50K+ per year. Most come with a service obligation (federal employment after graduation). If you're willing to work for the federal government for a few years, these are life-changing.

CyberCorps: Scholarship for Service
Full ride
NSF / OPM · Tuition + $27K–$37K stipend · Service required

The gold standard federal cyber scholarship. Covers full tuition plus a substantial living stipend. Service obligation: work for federal government for the same number of years you were funded. Apply through your school's participating program — not every school has SFS.

sfs.opm.gov
DoD Cyber Scholarship Program (CySP)
Full ride
DoD · Undergrad + grad · Civilian DoD service obligation

DoD's equivalent to CyberCorps — full tuition plus stipend in exchange for civilian DoD employment after graduation. Specifically focused on CAE-designated schools (like Lone Star College and others with NSA/CISA designation).

dodcio.defense.gov/Cyber-Workforce/CySP
SANS Scholarship Programs
Training + cert
SANS · Diversity, Veterans, Women's Immersion · No tuition

SANS runs multiple scholarship programs covering their $8K+ training courses plus GIAC certs — effectively $10K+ of training free. Programs are targeted (diversity, veterans, etc.) so read eligibility carefully before applying.

sans.org/cybertalent/scholarships
(ISC)² Scholarships
Tuition + cert
Center for Cyber Safety & Education · Multiple programs · Annual

(ISC)² offers scholarships for undergrads, grad students, women, and career-changers. Awards range from $1,000 to full tuition and often include free certification vouchers. Applications typically open spring, close summer.

isc2.org/scholarships
Community College Cyber Pilot (C3)
Full ride
NSF · Community college focused · Federal employment

CyberCorps-style funding specifically for community college students. You don't need to be at a 4-year school to qualify. Covers tuition plus stipend plus a federal placement after graduation. Check if your community college participates.

sfs.opm.gov/ContactsPI.aspx
Texas Grant Programs
State
Texas Workforce Commission · TEXAS Grant · TEOG · State-level aid

Texas residents have access to TEXAS Grant, Texas Educational Opportunity Grant (TEOG), and workforce training scholarships. Not cyber-specific, but stackable with federal aid. Start with your FAFSA and your school's financial aid office.

collegeforalltexans.com

// 07 · Résumés & LinkedIn

Get past the ATS.

An ATS (Applicant Tracking System) is the software companies use to automatically filter résumés before a human ever sees them. It scans for specific keywords matching the job posting — and rejects anything that doesn't match. That means your résumé isn't really for humans first. It's for the software.

Most résumés are rejected before a recruiter ever opens them, because they fail keyword matching, have weird formatting, or don't look like the job posting.

The entry-level cyber résumé — what actually works

One page. You don't have the experience for two. No photos, no graphics, no tables, no columns — ATS systems mangle all of these.

Match the posting. If the job says "Security+ required," make sure "Security+" appears word-for-word in your résumé. ATS keyword matching is simple string matching — write it the way it appears in the job ad.

Lead with what you've done, not what you know. A bullet point like "Built Active Directory lab with 3 VMs and used BloodHound to identify attack paths" beats "Familiar with Active Directory" every single time. Your home lab work goes under a "Projects" section — treat it like real experience because it is.

Certs go at the top. If you have Security+ or Splunk Core, it goes near your name, not buried at the bottom. That's the first thing recruiters look for.

Include a GitHub link. Even if it just has your home lab writeups and some Python scripts. Silence is worse than imperfection — a thin GitHub beats no GitHub.

// 08 · Interview prep

Earn the offer.

Most entry-level cyber interviews have three parts: behavioral questions, technical questions, and a scenario walk-through. You can practice every one of them.

The STAR method — use this for behavioral questions

Most behavioral questions start with "Tell me about a time when..." The best answers follow a structure: Situation, Task, Action, Result.

  • Situation — brief context (1–2 sentences, not a history lesson)
  • Task — what you specifically had to do
  • Action — the steps YOU took (most of the answer lives here)
  • Result — outcome, ideally with a measurable impact

Prepare 5–7 stories you can adapt — from your home lab, CTFs, school projects, part-time jobs, or student organizations. Most interviewees ramble. Structured answers stand out.

Questions to actually rehearse

Behavioral (STAR questions)

  • Tell me about a time you learned something technical on your own
  • Describe a time you solved a problem without help
  • Tell me about a conflict with a teammate and how you handled it
  • Describe a time you failed at something and what you learned
  • Walk me through a project you're proud of
  • Why cybersecurity? (have a specific, non-generic answer)

Technical — networking & fundamentals

  • Walk me through what happens when I type google.com into a browser
  • What's the difference between TCP and UDP?
  • Explain how DNS works and where it can be abused
  • What's the difference between symmetric and asymmetric encryption?
  • What is the CIA triad? Give an example of each
  • How does HTTPS work? What's a TLS handshake?

Technical — security-specific

  • Explain the difference between authentication and authorization
  • What's a buffer overflow? What does it actually do?
  • What is SQL injection and how do you prevent it?
  • Explain the kill chain or MITRE ATT&CK framework
  • What is defense in depth?
  • What's the difference between IDS and IPS?
  • What is zero trust?

SOC-specific

  • Walk me through how you would investigate a suspicious login alert
  • What's the difference between a false positive and a true positive?
  • What would make you suspect a phishing email?
  • How would you triage 50 open alerts at the start of a shift?
  • What log sources would you want access to as a tier-1 analyst?
  • Describe what a SIEM does and name two you've used or studied

"Show me" technical questions

  • Walk me through something you built in your home lab
  • Describe a CTF challenge you solved and how you approached it
  • What's the last article/paper/blog post you read about security?
  • What tool do you use that most people don't know about?
  • Show me your GitHub

Questions YOU should ask them

  • What does a typical day look like in this role?
  • What tools/SIEM does the team use?
  • How does the team handle on-call and after-hours?
  • What does success look like in this role after 6 months?
  • What's the team structure and who would I report to?
  • Are there opportunities to learn new areas (pentest, DFIR, etc.)?

// Important · Read this

Don't hack what you don't have permission to hack.

The Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA) is broad, federal, and not forgiving. Unauthorized access to computer systems — even "just looking" — is a crime. Your home lab, HackTheBox, TryHackMe, and other platforms that explicitly invite you to hack are safe. Your neighbor's Wi-Fi, your school's network, and sites with bug bounty programs before you read their rules are not.

If you find a vulnerability in a system you don't own, responsible disclosure is the path. Report it to the owner, give them time to fix it, and do not publish details until they've patched. Bug bounty programs on HackerOne and Bugcrowd codify this — read the scope before testing anything.
